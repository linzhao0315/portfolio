find their future.

The real problem: students don't all start from the same place

Before the sprint, I ran a moderated study with prospective students — testing two of our tools against two competitors. The key finding wasn't about features. It was about readiness:

The Decided — "I know what I want"
  • Clearly knows the specific program and job
  • Wants to skip assessments and go straight to the program finder
The Explorer — "I have a general idea"
  • Has a general idea of the field of study
  • Wants career-to-program matches without lengthy assessments
The Lost — "I'm unsure"
  • Unsure about both what to study and which career
  • Prefers detailed assessments covering career, skills, interests

Core insight: A single flow can't serve all three. The Decided wants to skip straight to program search. The Explorer wants career-to-program matching. The Lost needs a guided assessment. One tool, forked experiences.

Five days, five phases, one room

I pulled 9 stakeholders — execs, PMs, engineers, SEO, legal — into an intensive sprint. The constraint was the point: no time for politics, no room for scope creep.

Day 1
Map — Goals, questions, and HMW
Started with fears — "How could this fail?" — then converted them into sprint questions. From there, the team voted on HMW priorities:
  • Create a fun and inviting tool to maximize engagement
  • Provide career info to address users' major pain points
  • Bridge skill gaps with relevant programs
  • Align the tool with employer needs (skills, job matches, top traits)
  • Offer customized paths for diverse users
  • Develop an ecosystem for users' long-term career growth
Individual mapping, then convergence. The decision maker's final call: focus on two flows — Career Search (for Explorers and the Lost) and Program Finder (for the Decided).
Day 2
Sketch — Lightning demos & concept creation
The team researched inspiring products, captured screenshots, and shared patterns. Lightning demos surfaced the ideas that seeded Day 3's concepts.
Day 3
Decide — Art Museum & feature selection
Silent review, dot voting, speed critiques. The room converged on features for each flow:

Program Finder (for the Decided)

  • Filterable catalog with real-time cost, salary, and career pathway data
  • Skip the assessment — go straight to searching

Career Assessment (for Explorers + the Lost)

  • 2-4 min guided assessment with animated engagement cues
  • Results: top traits, matched careers, projected growth, and program fit
Day 4
Prototype — Two high-fidelity prototypes
Two high-fidelity prototypes were built in one day — one for Career Assessment, one for Program Finder. I conducted a dry run with the interviewer and decision maker to identify issues and prepare for Day 5 testing.
Day 5 + 1 Week
Test & Iterate
Usability testing with real users revealed targeted improvements needed for each flow:

Program Finder Iterations

  • Include quick facts (cost, credits, program length)
  • Add icons/pictures for each program
  • Implement autofill for search bar

Career Assessment Iterations

  • Use direct, clear questions to avoid confusion
  • Add animated emojis for engagement
  • Shorten results page text, use bullet points

What five days bought

The sprint didn't just produce prototypes — it produced alignment. Nine stakeholders walked out of that room with the same mental model of the product, the same prioritization, and the same evidence base.

The real lesson: Sprints aren't about speed — they're about compression of ambiguity. The forked experience design (two distinct flows serving three persona types) would have taken months to negotiate through normal process. Five days of structured constraint forced decisions that committee meetings never would.
